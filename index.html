A Heuristic Aided Stochastic Beam Search Algorithm for Solving the Transit Network Design Problem
Authors: Kazi Ashik Islam, Ibraheem Muhammad Moosa, Jaiaid Mobin, Muhammad Ali Nayeem and M. Sohel Rahman
Current Status: Submitted to Swarm and Evolutionary Computation, Elsevier (Under Review)

Summary: Designing efficient transit networks that fulfils the demand of passengers while maintaining constraints on the size of the network is a computationally hard problem. It can be thought of as a combinatorial optimization problem. Previous research focused on applying metaheuristic techniques such as Genetic Algorithm, Bee Colony Algorithm etc.

We designed a novel heuristic that can find good transit networks given the travel time and demand among bus terminals and constraints on number of routes and route length. Then we designed a mutation operator based on this heuristic for stochastic beam search.

Our experiments on benchmark datasets show that our heuristic significantly outperforms the previous metaheuristic approaches. In particular our heuristic which runs in seconds significatly utperforms the state of the art metaheuristic algorithms that takes hours to run. Our proposed stochastic beam search finds even better solutions while requiring computational resources similar to state of the art metaheuristic algorithms.

Predicting B-factors From Protein Sequence using Deep Learning
Authors: A. S. M. Ahsan-Ul-Haque, Ibraheem Muhammad Moosa, M. Sohel Rahman
Current Status: Ongoing

Summary: Tertiary structure of proteins are determined using X-ray Crytallography or NMR Spectroscopy. A byproduct of X-ray Crystallography is the B-factor which is a measure of uncertainty in the determined positions of the atoms in the tertiary structure. One major source of this uncertainty is thermal motions of atoms. Thus the b-factor of C-alpha atoms of the amino acids in a protein sequence can be a good indicator of protein flexibility.

Since X-ray Crytallography is a costly experiment and it is hard to crystalize many proteins, it is an interesting problem to predict a normalized b-factor for C-alpha atoms of each amino acid from the protein sequence alone. It is to be noted that the gap between the number of proteins discovered and the number of proteins for which X-ray Crytallography is performed is increasing every year.

From a theoretical point of view this is a sequence to sequence learning problem. Here the input sequence is categorical (each element of the sequence is one of the twenty standard amino acids). And the output sequence is real valued.

Thus Recurrent Neural Networks which can deal with variable length sequences of input and output are very much approprate for this problem. We applied Bidirectional LSTM Network to this problem and found better results than previous approaches that only used sequence data.

We note that none of the previous approaches applied deep learning. Also rather than considering the variable length sequence nature of this problem they considered only fixed length windows of the sequence and applied classical machine learning algorithms (SVR, Random Forest etc).

We are currently writing a paper describing our findings. Our future plan includes applying Convolutional Neural Networks which are much faster than RNNs and can deal with variable length input when the input and out sizes are same (as in our case). Also previous studies found that using evolutionary information in addition to the sequence significantly improves prediction. We plan to include these information in our model.
